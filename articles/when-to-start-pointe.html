When Is My Child Ready for Pointe?

A practical guide for parents navigating one of ballet's biggest milestones

If your child dances ballet, the question will eventually come up: "When can I go on pointe?" For many young dancers, pointe shoes represent a rite of passage — a visible marker that they've reached a new level. And for parents, it can be an anxious moment. You want to support your child's passion, but you also want to make sure they're safe.

The short answer is that most dancers begin pointe work between ages 11 and 13, after at least two to three years of consistent ballet training. But age alone doesn't determine readiness. A combination of physical maturity, strength, technique, and training history all factor into the decision — and ultimately, your child's ballet teacher is the best person to make that call.

This guide covers everything parents need to know: the physical requirements, what training your child should have under their belt, how the assessment process works, and what those first months on pointe actually look like.

Why Age Alone Isn't Enough

You'll sometimes hear a specific number thrown around — 12 is common — as though there's a magic birthday that unlocks pointe work. In reality, age is just a rough proxy for skeletal maturity. The bones in a child's foot contain growth plates that haven't fully ossified (hardened) yet. Dancing on pointe before these growth plates close can cause permanent damage to the developing foot.

Most pediatric orthopedists agree that the bones in the foot are sufficiently mature somewhere around age 11 to 13, but this varies from child to child. Some children's feet mature earlier, some later. A 12-year-old who started ballet six months ago is not ready for pointe. A 13-year-old with four years of solid training and strong feet may be more than ready.

Key takeaway: Age is a necessary minimum threshold, not a green light. Think of it as one checkbox among several, all of which need to be checked before your child goes on pointe.

Physical Readiness: What Teachers Look For

When a ballet teacher evaluates whether a student is ready for pointe, they're assessing several specific physical qualities. Here's what matters most:

Bone Maturity

As mentioned above, the growth plates in the foot need to be sufficiently developed. In some cases, a teacher may recommend an X-ray or consultation with a sports medicine professional if there's any doubt. This is especially relevant for dancers who are tall for their age or who entered puberty later than average.

Ankle Strength and Stability

Pointe work places enormous demand on the ankles. A dancer needs to be able to rise to full demi-pointe (the ball of the foot) on one leg and hold that position with stability — no wobbling, no rolling inward or outward. If a dancer can't maintain clean, steady relevés in soft shoes, they're not ready for the additional challenge of a pointe shoe.

Core Strength and Alignment

Pointe is not just about the feet. A dancer needs strong abdominal and back muscles to "pull up" out of the shoes rather than sinking into them. Good alignment — ears over shoulders, shoulders over hips, hips over ankles — is essential. Without it, a dancer on pointe is essentially balancing on a tiny platform with no muscular support, which is a recipe for injury.

Intrinsic Foot Strength

The small muscles inside the foot do critical work on pointe. Teachers look for the ability to articulate through the foot — rolling smoothly through demi-pointe rather than hopping up and down — and the ability to maintain the arch of the foot under load. A dancer with "knuckling" (collapsing over the toe box) lacks the foot strength for safe pointe work.

Turnout Control

A dancer should be able to maintain their turnout from the hips, not by forcing the feet. On pointe, any compensation or misalignment is magnified. A student who can hold a clean, well-aligned passé on flat or demi-pointe is demonstrating the kind of control that transfers to pointe work.

Training Prerequisites

Physical ability doesn't develop in a vacuum. It comes from consistent training over time. Most schools and professional organizations recommend the following minimums before a student begins pointe:

  • 2–3 years of consistent ballet training — not casual once-a-week classes, but dedicated study with a qualified teacher
  • At least 2–3 ballet classes per week at the time pointe is introduced, so the dancer has regular reinforcement of technique
  • Solid proficiency in basic ballet vocabulary — clean pliés, tendu, relevé, passé, and basic pirouettes on flat
  • Experience with center work — the student should be comfortable working away from the barre, demonstrating balance and spatial awareness

A student who takes one ballet class per week, even for several years, typically doesn't build the strength or muscle memory needed for pointe. Frequency matters as much as duration.

Trust the teacher. A good ballet instructor knows what to look for and will recommend pointe when — and only when — a student is genuinely ready. If your child's teacher says "not yet," that's a protective decision, not a criticism of your child's ability.

The Role of the Ballet Teacher

This is worth emphasizing: the decision to start pointe should be made by a qualified ballet teacher, not by the student, not by the parent, and not by a peer group. A trained teacher sees your child in class regularly and understands their individual strengths and weaknesses in a way that no one else can.

Be cautious of any studio that puts students on pointe based solely on age or that allows parents to request it. Studios that take a careful, individualized approach to pointe readiness are protecting your child's long-term health. The best programs will often have a formal assessment process.

What a Pre-Pointe Assessment Looks Like

Many studios conduct a structured evaluation before clearing a student for pointe. While the specifics vary, a typical pre-pointe assessment might include:

  • Relevé test: The dancer rises to demi-pointe on one foot and holds for a set number of counts, demonstrating ankle stability and strength
  • Plié and alignment check: The teacher observes whether the knees track over the toes and the hips remain level
  • Core stability exercises: Simple tests like holding a balance in passé or performing slow, controlled movements at the barre
  • Foot articulation: The teacher watches how the student rolls through the foot during tendus and relevés
  • Range of motion: Checking ankle flexibility (both plantarflexion and dorsiflexion) to ensure the foot can achieve a safe position on pointe

Some studios also work with physical therapists or sports medicine professionals who specialize in dance. If your school offers or recommends a professional screening, take advantage of it — it provides an extra layer of confidence that your child is truly ready.

What to Expect in the First Weeks and Months

Starting pointe is exciting, but it's important to have realistic expectations. The early weeks are focused on fundamentals, and the work is harder than most students anticipate.

The first few classes will likely be spent at the barre, learning to rise to pointe (relevé) with two hands for support. Students practice going up and coming down slowly, building the specific muscles used in pointe work. There's no jumping, no turning, and no center work yet.

The first few months gradually introduce more exercises: échappés (opening and closing the feet on pointe), simple bourrées, and eventually pas de bourrée. It's a slow, methodical progression, and it should be. Rushing through these stages increases injury risk significantly.

Soreness is normal. Your child may experience aching toes, blisters, and general foot fatigue in the early weeks. This is part of the adjustment process. However, sharp or persistent pain is not normal and should be reported to the teacher immediately.

Most students begin with one pointe class per week in addition to their regular ballet classes. Over time, as strength builds, more pointe work is incorporated.

Common Concerns Parents Have

Will my child get injured?

Pointe work carries inherent risk, but when introduced at the right time and with proper instruction, serious injuries are uncommon. The most common issues are blisters, bruised toenails, and minor aches — uncomfortable but manageable. Stress fractures, sprains, and tendon injuries are more often associated with starting too early, training too intensely, or wearing poorly fitted shoes. A qualified teacher and properly fitted shoes are your best protection.

Is the pain normal?

Some discomfort is expected, especially in the first weeks as the feet adapt. Pointe will never feel like wearing slippers. But there's a clear line between discomfort and pain. If your child dreads putting on their shoes, feels sharp pain, or has pain that persists after class ends, something is wrong — usually a fitting issue or a need for more strength-building before continuing.

What about the cost?

This is a practical concern that catches many families off guard. Pointe shoes typically cost $80 to $120 per pair, and they wear out. A beginner dancing on pointe once a week might get two to three months from a pair. More advanced students who dance several days a week may go through shoes every few weeks. Budget for multiple pairs per year.

Beyond the shoes themselves, students may need toe pads, toe spacers, and other accessories. Check out our guide to the best pointe shoes for recommendations at various price points.

On cost: Don't buy pointe shoes online for your child's first pair. The initial fitting should always be done in person at a reputable dance supply store by an experienced fitter. A poorly fitted shoe is the number one cause of preventable pointe-related problems. See our pointe shoe fitting guide for what to expect.

My child's friends are starting pointe and she isn't — what do I say?

This is one of the hardest situations for parents. Peer pressure is real, and young dancers can feel left out when classmates advance to pointe before they do. Remind your child that readiness is individual, not competitive. A student who starts pointe before they're ready is more likely to struggle with pain, frustration, and injury — which can end a dance career faster than waiting an extra six months.

Red Flags to Watch For

While most ballet schools handle pointe responsibly, be alert to these warning signs:

  • A studio that starts students on pointe before age 10 or 11
  • No individual assessment — students advance to pointe based solely on age or class level
  • Large class sizes with minimal individual attention during pointe work
  • A teacher who dismisses your child's pain complaints
  • No requirement for professional shoe fitting

If any of these apply, it may be worth seeking a second opinion from another qualified ballet teacher or a dance medicine professional.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can boys do pointe?

Yes. While pointe work is traditionally part of female ballet training, some male dancers study pointe for cross-training benefits, for roles that require it (some contemporary choreography), or simply out of interest. The readiness criteria are the same regardless of gender.

My child does other dance styles but not much ballet. Can they start pointe?

Pointe readiness is specifically about ballet technique. Jazz, contemporary, lyrical, and other styles build different movement patterns. A dancer needs dedicated ballet training — barre work, proper alignment, classical technique — before they're ready for pointe, regardless of their experience in other forms.

Should I get an X-ray of my child's feet before starting pointe?

This isn't routinely necessary, but some parents and teachers request it for peace of mind, especially if there's concern about early skeletal maturity. A pediatric orthopedist or sports medicine doctor can assess bone development. If your child's teacher recommends it, it's worth doing.

How long does a pair of pointe shoes last?

For beginners, typically 2 to 4 months. For intermediate students dancing several times a week, 4 to 8 weeks. Professional dancers may go through a pair in a single performance. The shank (the supportive sole) softens with use, and once the shoe loses its structure, it no longer provides adequate support. Check our pointe shoe guide for durable options that offer good value.

What if my child starts pointe and wants to stop?

That's completely fine. Some dancers try pointe and decide it isn't for them, or they may want to take a break and return later. There's no obligation to continue, and stepping back from pointe doesn't mean stepping back from ballet.

Can my child practice pointe at home?

Not without direct teacher supervision, especially in the early stages. Practicing pointe without proper guidance is one of the fastest ways to develop bad habits or sustain an injury. Home exercises like relevés on flat, theraband work, and balance exercises are great for building strength, but actual pointe work should happen in class.

The Bottom Line

Starting pointe is a milestone worth celebrating — when the timing is right. The best thing you can do as a parent is find a reputable ballet school, trust the teacher's professional judgment, ensure your child gets a proper fitting, and keep the lines of communication open so your child feels comfortable reporting any pain or concerns.

Your child's feet have to last a lifetime. A few extra months of patience now protects decades of dancing ahead.
